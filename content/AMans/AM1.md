{{<exercise 1>}}
Let $x$ be a nilpotent element of a ring. Show that $1 + x$ is a unit of $A$. Deduce that the sum of a nilpotent element and a unit is a unit. 
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
If $x^n=0$, then $(1+x)^{-1}=1-x+\cdots+(-1)^{n-1}x^{n-1}$. The second statement is thus obvious.

{{<exercise 2>}}
Let $A$ be a ring and let $A[x]$ be the ring of polynomials in an indeterminate $x$, with coefficients in $A$. Let $f=a_0+a_1x+\cdots+a_nx^n\in A[x]$. Prove that
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
i) $f$ is a unit in $A[x]\Leftrightarrow a_0$ is a unit in $A$ and $a_1,\ldots,a_n$ are nilpotent. [If $b_0+b_1x+\cdots+b_mx^m$ is the inverse of $f$, prove by induction on $r$ that $a_n^{r+1}b_{m-r}=0$. Hence show that $a_n$ is nilpotent, and then use Ex. 1.]
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
$\Rightarrow$: Following the hint, the induction step is by multiplying $a_n^r$ on the left of \[\sum_{i=0}^ra_{n-i}b_{m-r+i}=0\] and once we know $a_n$ is nilpotent, we subtract $a_nx^n$ from $f$ and do the same analysis for $a_{n-1}$, etc., because the rest is still a unit by Exercise 1.\
$\Leftarrow$: Exercise 1.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
ii) $f$ is nilpotent $\Leftrightarrow a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_n$ are nilpotent.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Both directions use the fact that the sum of nilpotents is nilpotent. If $f$ is nilpotent, the leading term coefficient $a_n$ must also be nilpotent, so we subtract it from $f$ and analyze similarly.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iii) $f$ is a zero-divisor $\Leftrightarrow$ there exists $a\neq0$ in $A$ such that $af=0$. [Choose a polynomial $g=b_0+b_1x+\cdots+b_mx^m$ of least degree $m$ such that $fg=0$. Then $a_nb_m=0$, hence $a_ng=0$ (because $a_ng$ annihilates $f$ and has degree $< m$). Now show by induction that $a_{n-r}g=0(0\le r\le n)$.]
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
$\Rightarrow$: Following the hint, the induction step is still by \[\sum_{i=0}^ra_{n-i}b_{m-r+i}=0\] from which we get $a_{n-r}g=0$, hence $b_0f=0$.\
$\Leftarrow$: Obvious.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iv) $f$ is said to be *primitive* if $(a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_n) = (1)$. Prove that if $f,g\in A[x]$, then $fg$ is primitive $\Leftrightarrow f$ and $g$ are primitive. {{<explain>}}The special case where $A=\mathbf{Z}$ is called Gauss's lemma.{{</explain>}}
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Denote $c(f)=(a_0,\ldots,a_n)$, and $g=b_0+b_1x+\cdots+b_mx^m$.\
$\Rightarrow$: By proof of contradiction, without loss of generality assume $f$ is not primitive, take maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}\supseteq c(f)$, then $fg$ cannot be primitive because \[c(fg)\subseteq c(f)c(g)\subseteq\mathfrak{m}A=\mathfrak{m}\] here $c(f)c(g)$ is the product of ideals, i.e. the ideal generated by all elements of the form $ab$ with $a\in c(f)$, $b\in c(g)$.\
$\Leftarrow$: By proof of contradiction, if $fg$ is not primitive, take maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}\supseteq c(fg)$. Descend $f$, $g$ to $(A/\mathfrak{m})[x]$, so that $\overline{f}\cdot\overline{g}=0$, but the polynomial ring over a field is integral, hence without loss of generality $\overline{f}=0$, i.e. $c(f)\subseteq\mathfrak{m}$, so $f$ is not primitive.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 5>}}
Let $A$ be a ring and let $A[[x]]$ be the ring of formal power series $f=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}a_nx^n$ with coefficients in $A$. Show that
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
i) $f$ is a unit in $A[[x]]\Leftrightarrow a_0$ is a unit in $A$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
$\Rightarrow$ is obvious. For $\Leftarrow$, by multiplying with $a_0^{-1}$ we may assume $a_0=1$, then we can determine recursively the coefficients of $f^{-1}$ by \[(1+\bullet)^{-1}=1-\bullet+\bullet^2-\cdots\] 
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
ii) If $f$ is nilpotent, then $a_n$ is nilpotent for all $n\ge0$. Is the converse true? (See Chapter 7, Exercise 2.)
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The same as Exercise 2 ii), except that here we consider from the lowest degree term.\
The converse is not true. The idea is to let the coefficients hve increasing order of nilpotency, for example consider {{<explain>}}very likely not the simplest solution{{</explain>}} \[A=\mathbf{Z}[t,t^{\frac12},t^{\frac13},\ldots]/(t),\quad f=tx+t^{\frac12}x^2+t^{\frac13}x^3+\cdots\in A[[x]]\] whose coefficients are clearly nilpotent. We prove that $f^m$ has a nonzero term $mt^{1-\frac1{m(m+1)}}x^{m^2+1}$, i.e., there are only $m$ ordered pairs $(a_1,\cdots,a_m)\in\mathbf{Z}_+^m$ such that \[a_1+\cdots+a_m=m^2+1,\quad \frac1{a_1}+\cdots+\frac1{a_m}=1-\frac1{m(m+1)}\] Without loss of generality assume $a_m$ is the largest of them, which must be $\ge m+1$, we have \[\begin{align*}
\frac1{a_1}+\cdots+\frac1{a_m}&=\frac1{a_1}+\cdots+\frac1{a_{m-1}}+\frac1{a_m-1}-\frac1{a_m(a_m-1)}\\&\ge\frac{m^2}{a_1+\cdots+a_{m-1}+a_m-1}-\frac1{a_m(a_m-1)}\ge1-\frac1{m(m+1)}
\end{align*}\] hence this achieves equality everywhere, forcing $a_1,\ldots,a_m$ to have $m-1$ of them equal to $m$ and one equal to $m+1$, which gives a term $mt^{1-\frac1{m(m+1)}}x^{m^2+1}$ in $f^m$. Therefore $f$ is not nilpotent.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iii) $f$ belongs to the Jacobson radical of $A[[x]]\Leftrightarrow a_0$ belongs to the Jacobson radical of $A$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Saying $f$ is in the Jacobson radical is equivalent to saying for any $g=b_0+b_1x+\cdots$, $1-gf$ is invertible {{<explain>}}Proposition 1.9{{</explain>}}, which is equivalent to saying for any $b_0$, $1-b_0a_0$ is invertible {{<explain>}}Exercise 5, i){{</explain>}}, which is equivalent to $a_0$ being in the Jacobson radical of $A$ {{<explain>}}Again Proposition 1.9{{</explain>}}.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iv) The contraction of a maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$ of $A[[x]]$ is a maximal ideal of $A$, and $\mathfrak{m}$ is generated by $\mathfrak{m}^c$ and $x$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
We obviously have $\mathfrak{m}\subseteq\mathfrak{m}^c+(x)\subset A[[x]]$, by maximality $\mathfrak{m}=\mathfrak{m}^c+(x)$. Therefore $A/\mathfrak{m}^c=A[[x]]/(\mathfrak{m}^c+(x))=A[[x]]/\mathfrak{m}$ is a field, hence $\mathfrak{m}^c$ is maximal.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
v) Every prime ideal of $A$ is the contraction of a prime ideal of $A[[x]]$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ is the contraction of the prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}+(x)$.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 7>}}
Let $A$ be a ring in which every element $x$ satisfies $x^n=x$ for some $n > 1$ (depending on $x$). Show that every prime ideal in $A$ is maximal.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
For prime $\mathfrak{p}$, $A/\mathfrak{p}$ inherits this property, but it is integral, hence every nonzero $x\in A/\mathfrak{p}$ satisfies $x\cdot x^{n-2}=1$, i.e. $A/\mathfrak{p}$ is a field, making $\mathfrak{p}$ maximal.

### *Construction of an algebraic closure of a field* (E. Artin)

{{<exercise 13>}}
Let $K$ be a field and let $\Sigma$ be the set of all irreducible monic polynomials $f$ in one indeterminate with coefficients in $K$. Let $A$ be the polynomial ring over $K$ generated by indeterminates $x_f$, one for each $f\in \Sigma$. Let $\mathfrak{a}$ be the ideal of $A$ generated by the polynomials $f(x_f)$ for all $f\in \Sigma$. Show that $\mathfrak{a}\neq(1)$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
If not, take $n$ minimal such that there is finite sum \[\sum_{i=1}^na_{f_i}f_i(x_{f_i})=1,\quad a_{f_i}\in A\] which means that in the ring $B=K[x_{f_1},\ldots,x_{f_n}]$ we have \[\mathfrak{b}=(f_1(x_{f_1}),\ldots,f_n(x_{f_n}))=(1)\] Consider the ring $C=K[x_{f_1},\ldots,x_{f_{n-1}}]\subset B$ and by minimality of $n$ \[\mathfrak{c}=(f_1(x_{f_1}),\ldots,f_{n-1}(x_{f_{n-1}}))\neq C\] notice that $\mathfrak{b}=\mathfrak{c}[x_{f_n}]+(f_n(x_{f_n}))$, but by $\deg f_n\ge2$ we get a contradiction: \[0=B/\mathfrak{b}=C[x_{f_n}]/(\mathfrak{c}[x_{f_n}]+(f_n(x_{f_n})))=(C/\mathfrak{c})[x_{f_n}]/(f_n(x_{f_n}))\neq0\]
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
Let $\mathfrak{m}$ be a maximal ideal of $A$ containing $\mathfrak{a}$, and let $K_1=A/\mathfrak{m}$. Then $K_1$ is an extension field of $K$ in which each $f\in \Sigma$ has a root. Repeat the construction with $K_1$ in place of $K$, obtaining a field $K_2$, and so on. Let $L=\bigcup\limits_{n=1}^{\infty}K_n$. Then $L$ is a field in which each $f\in\Sigma$ splits completely into linear factors. Let $\overline{K}$ be the set of all elements of $L$ which are algebraic over $K$. Then $\overline{K}$ is an algebraic closure of $K$.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 14>}}
In a ring $A$, let $\Sigma$ be the set of all ideals in which every element is a zero-divisor. Show that the set $\Sigma$ has maximal elements and that every maximal element of $\Sigma$ is a prime ideal. Hence the set of zero-divisors in $A$ is a union of prime ideals.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The existence is a standard application of Zorn's lemma. For maximal element $\mathfrak{a}$ and $x_1,x_2\not\in\mathfrak{a}$, by maximality there exists $y_1,y_2\in A,z_1,z_2\in\mathfrak{a}$ such that $x_1y_1+z_1$, $x_2y_2+z_2$ are both not zero-divisors. Their product is also not a zero-divisor, but it is in $(x_1x_2)+\mathfrak{a}$, hence $x_1x_2\not\in\mathfrak{a}$.
{{</exercise>}}

### *The prime spectrum of a ring*

{{<exercise 15>}}
Let $A$ be a ring and let $X$ be the set of all prime ideals of $A$. For each subset $E$ of $A$, let $V(E)$ denote the set of all prime ideals of $A$ which contain $E$. Prove that
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
i) if $\mathfrak{a}$ is the ideal generated by $E$, then $V(E)=V(\mathfrak{a})=V(r(\mathfrak{a}))$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
As an ideal, $\mathfrak{p}\supseteq E\Leftrightarrow \mathfrak{p}\supseteq\mathfrak{a}$. As a prime ideal, $\mathfrak{p}\supseteq\mathfrak{a}\Leftrightarrow\mathfrak{p}\supseteq r(\mathfrak{a})$.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
ii) $V(0)=X$, $V(1)=\varnothing$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Obvious.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iii) If $(E_i)_{i\in I}$ is any family of subsets of $A$, then \[ V(\bigcup_{i\in I} E_i)=\bigcap_{i\in I}V(E_i). \]
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Obvious.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iv) $V(\mathfrak{a}\cap\mathfrak{b})=V(\mathfrak{ab})=V(\mathfrak{a})\cup V(\mathfrak{b})$ for any ideals $\mathfrak{a}$, $\mathfrak{b}$ of $A$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
We have $V(\mathfrak{a}\cap \mathfrak{b})=V(r(\mathfrak{a}\cap\mathfrak{b}))=V(r(\mathfrak{ab}))=V(\mathfrak{ab})$ {{<explain>}}Exercise 1.13{{</explain>}}, and $\mathfrak{p}\supseteq \mathfrak{a}\cap\mathfrak{b}$ is equivalent to $\mathfrak{p}\supseteq \mathfrak{a}$ or $\mathfrak{b}$ {{<explain>}}Proposition 1.11 ii){{</explain>}}.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
These results show that the sets $V(E)$ satisfy the axioms for closed sets in a topological space. The resulting topology is called the *Zariski topology*. The topological space $X$ is called the *prime spectrum* of $A$, and is written $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 20>}}
Let $X$ be a topological space.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
i) If $Y$ is an irreducible (Exercise 19) subspace of $X$, then the closure $\overline{Y}$ of $Y$ in $X$ is irreducible.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Saying $Y$ is irreducible is equivalent to saying that for closed sets $V_1,V_2\subseteq X$, if $Y\subseteq V_1\cup V_2$, then $Y\subseteq V_1$ or $Y\subseteq V_2$. Thus the question is obvious because $Y\subseteq V\Leftrightarrow \overline{Y}\subseteq V$ for any closed set $V$.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
ii) Every irreducible subspace of $X$ is contained in a maximal irreducible subspace.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
A standard application of Zorn's lemma.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\

They are singletons, because any two points can be separated by 'non-intersecting' open sets.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\

{{<exercise 21>}}
Let $\phi:A\to B$ be a ring homomorphism. Let $X=\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ and $Y=\operatorname{Spec}(B)$. If $\mathfrak{q}\in Y$, then $\phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})$ is a prime ideal of $A$, i.e., a point of $X$. Hence $\phi$ induces a mapping $\phi^*:Y\to X$. Show that
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
i) If $f\in A$ then ${\phi^*}^{-1}(X_f)=Y_{\phi(f)}$, and hence that $\phi^*$ is continuous.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
$\mathfrak{q}\in Y_{\phi(f)}\Leftrightarrow \phi(f)\not\in\mathfrak{q}\Leftrightarrow f\not\in\phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})\Leftrightarrow \phi^*(\mathfrak{q})\in X_f$.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
ii) If $\mathfrak{a}$ is an ideal of $A$, then ${\phi^*}^{-1}(V(\mathfrak{a}))=V(\mathfrak{a}^e)$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
$\mathfrak{q}\in V(\mathfrak{a}^e)\Leftrightarrow \phi^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})\supseteq\mathfrak{a}$.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iii) If $\mathfrak{b}$ is an ideal of $B$, then $\overline{\phi^*(V(\mathfrak{b}))}=V(\mathfrak{b}^c)$.

Let $L$ be a lattice, in which the $\text{sup}$ and $\text{inf}$ of two elements $a$, $b$ are denoted by $a\vee b$ and $a\wedge b$ respectively. $L$ is a *Boolean lattice* (or *Boolean algebra*) if\
i) $L$ has a least element and a greatest element (denoted by $0$, $1$ respectively).\
ii) Each of $\vee$, $\wedge$ is distributive over the other.\
iii) Each $a\in L$ has a unique "complement" $a'\in L$ such that $a\vee a'=1$ and $a\wedge a'=0$.\
(For example, the set of all subsets of a set, ordered by inclusion, is a Boolean lattice.)\
Let $L$ be a Boolean lattice. Define addition and multiplication in $L$ by the rules \[a+b=(a\wedge b')\vee(a'\wedge b),\quad ab=a\wedge b. \]
Verify that in this way $L$ becomes a Boolean ring, say $A(L)$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The only nontrivial verifications are:
- Additive inverse: $a+a=0$;
- Associativity of addition: Notice $(a\vee c)'=a'\wedge c'$ and $(a\wedge c)'=a'\vee c'$, then \[\begin{align*}a+b+c&=(((a\wedge b')\vee(a'\wedge b))\wedge c')\vee(((a\wedge b')\vee(a'\wedge b))'\wedge c)\\&=(a\wedge b'\wedge c')\vee(a'\wedge b\wedge c')\vee(a'\wedge b'\wedge c)\vee(a\wedge b\wedge c)\end{align*}\] which is symmetric in $a,b,c$, hence associativity.
- Distributivity of multiplication: we have \[\begin{align*}ab+ac&=((a\wedge b)\vee(a\wedge c))\wedge ((a\wedge b)'\vee(a\wedge c)')\\&=a\wedge (b\vee c)\wedge (a'\vee b'\vee c')\end{align*}\] from which $a$ can eliminate the $a'$ in the third term so that $ab+ac=a(b+c)$.

{{<exercise 26>}}
Let $A$ be a ring. The subspace of $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ consisting of the *maximal* ideals of $A$, with the induced topology, is called the *maximal spectrum* of $A$ and is denoted by $\operatorname{Max}(A)$. For arbitrary commutative rings it does not have the nice functorial properties of $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ (see Exercise 21), because the inverse image of a maximal ideal under a ring homomorphism need not be maximal.\
Let $X$ be a compact Hausdorff space and let $C(X)$ denote the ring of all real-valued continuous functions on $X$ (add and multiply functions by adding and multiplying their values). For each $x\in X$, let $\mathfrak{m}_x$ be the set of all $f\in C(X)$ such that $f(x)=0$. The ideal $\mathfrak{m}_x$ is maximal, because it is the kernel of the (surjective) homomorphism $C(X)\to\mathbf{R}$ which takes $f$ to $f(x)$. If $\widetilde{X}$ denotes $\operatorname{Max}(C(X))$, we have therefore defined a mapping $\mu:X\to\widetilde{X}$, namely $x\mapsto\mathfrak{m}_x$. We shall show that $\mu$ is a homeomorphism of $X$ onto $\widetilde{X}$.\
i) Let $\mathfrak{m}$ be any maximal ideal of $C(X)$, and let $V=V(\mathfrak{m})$ be the set of common zeros of the functions in $\mathfrak{m}$: that is, \[V=\{x\in X:f(x)=0\text{ for all }f\in \mathfrak{m}\}.\] Suppose that $V$ is empty. Then for each $x\in X$ there exists $f_x\in\mathfrak{m}$ such that $f_x(x)\neq0$. Since $f_x$ is continuous, there is an open neighborhood $U_x$ of $x$ in $X$ on which $f_x$ does not vanish. By compactness a finite number of the neighborhoods, say $U_{x_1},\ldots,U_{x_n}$, cover $X$. Let \[f=f_{x_1}^2+\cdots+f_{x_n}^2.\] Then $f$ does not vanish at any point of $X$, hence is a unit in $C(X)$. But this contradicts $f\in\mathfrak{m}$, hence $V$ is not empty.\
Let $x$ be a point of $V$. Thn $\mathfrak{m}\subseteq\mathfrak{m}_x$, hence $\mathfrak{m}=\mathfrak{m}_x$ because $\mathfrak{m}$ is maximal. Hence $\mu$ is surjective.\
ii) By Urysohn's lemma (this is the only non-trivial fact required in the argument) the continuous functions separate the points of $X$. Hence $x\neq y\Rightarrow\mathfrak{m}_x\neq\mathfrak{m}_y$, and therefore $\mu$ is injective.\
iii) Let $f\in C(X)$; let \[U_f=\{x\in X:f(x)\neq0\}\] and let \[\widetilde{U_f}=\{\mathfrak{m}\in\widetilde{X}:f\not\in\mathfrak{m}\}\] Show that $\mu(U_f)=\widetilde{U_f}$ {{<explain>}}They both say $f(x)$ is nonzero.{{</explain>}}. The open sets $U_f$ (resp. $\widetilde{U_f}$) form a basis of the topology of $X$ (resp. $\widetilde{X}$) and therefore $\mu$ is a homeomorphism.\
Thus $X$ can be reconstructed from the ring of functions $C(X)$.

### *Affine algebraic varieties*

{{<exercise 27>}}
Let $k$ be an algebraically closed field and let \[f_\alpha(t_1,\ldots,t_n)=0\] be a set of polynomial equations in $n$ variables with coefficients in $k$. The set $X$ of all points $x=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\in k^n$ which satisfy these equations is an *affine algebraic variety*.\
Consider the set of all polynomials $g\in k[t_1,\ldots,t_n]$ with the property that $g(x)=0$ for all $x\in X$. This set is an ideal $I(X)$ in the polynomial ring, and is called the *ideal of the variety $X$*. The quotient ring \[P(X)=k[t_1,\ldots,t_n]/I(X)\] is the ring of polynomial functions on $X$, because two polynomials $g$, $h$ define the same polynomial function on $X$ if and only if $g-h$ vanishes at every point of $X$, that is, if and only if $g-h\in I(X)$.\
Let $\xi_i$ be the image of $t_i$ in $P(X)$. The $\xi_i(1\le i\le n)$ are the *coordinate functions* on $X$: if $x\in X$, then $\xi_i(x)$ is the $i$th coordinate of $x$. $P(X)$ is generated as a $k$-algebra by the coordinate functions, and is called the *coordinate ring* (or affine algebra) of $X$.\
As in Exercise 26, for each $x\in X$ let $\mathfrak{m}_x$ be the ideal of all $f\in P(X)$ such that $f(x)=0$; it is a maximal ideal of $P(X)$. Hence, if $\widetilde{X}=\operatorname{Max}(P(X))$, we have defined a mapping $\mu:X\to\widetilde{X}$, namely $x\mapsto\mathfrak{m}_x$.\
It is easy to show that $\mu$ is injective: if $x\neq y$, we must have $x_i\neq y_i$ for some $i(1\le i\le n)$, and hence $\xi_i-x_i$ is in $\mathfrak{m}_x$ but not in $\mathfrak{m}_y$, so that $\mathfrak{m}_x\neq\mathfrak{m}_y$. What is less obvious (but still true) is that $\mu$ is *surjective*. This is one form of Hilbert's Nullstellensatz (see Chapter 7).
{{</exercise>}}

Let $f_1,\ldots,f_m$ be elements of $k[t_1,\ldots,t_n]$. They determine a *polynomial mapping* $\phi:k^n\to k^m$: if $x\in k^n$, the coordinates of $\phi(x)$ are $f_1(x),\ldots,f_m(x)$.\
Let $X$, $Y$ be affine algebraic varieties in $k^n$, $k^m$ respectively. A mapping $\phi:X\to Y$ is said to be *regular* if $\phi$ is the restriction to $X$ of a polynomial mapping from $k^n$ to $k^m$.\
