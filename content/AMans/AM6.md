{{<exercise 1>}}
i) Let $M$ be a Noetherian $A$-module and $u:M\to M$ a module homomorphism. If $u$ is surjective, then $u$ is an isomorphism.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The chain of submodules $\ker(u)\subseteq\ker(u^2)\subseteq\cdots$ must stabilize, so $\ker(u^n)=\ker(u^{n+1})$ for some $n$, i.e. $u^{n+1}x=0\Rightarrow u^nx=0$. Surjectivity enables us to find $u^nx=y$ for every $y\in M$, hence $uy=0\Rightarrow y=0$, i.e. $u$ is injective.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
ii) If $M$ is Artinian and $u$ is injective, then again $u$ is an isomorphism.\
[For (i), consider the submodules $\ker(u^n)$; for (ii), the quotient modules $\operatorname{coker}(u^n)$.]
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The proof is completely dual to i).
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 2>}}
Let $M$ be an $A$-module. If every non-empty set of finitely generated submodules of $M$ has a maximal element, then $M$ is Noetherian.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
By Proposition 6.2 we only need to prove that every submodule of $M$ is finitely generated; if not, we can pick an ascending chain of finitely generated submodules {{<explain>}}of some submodule of $M$ that is not finitely generated, by adding an element one at a time{{</explain>}} which does not stabilize.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 3>}}
Let $M$ be an $A$-module and let $N_1$, $N_2$ be submodules of $M$. If $M/N_1$ and $M/N_2$ are Noetherian, so is $M/(N_1\cap N_2)$. Similarly with Artinian in place of Noetherian.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The natural map $M/(N_1\cap N_2)\to M/N_1\oplus M/N_2$ embeds it to a submodule of a Noetherian module, hence Noether. The same for Artinian.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 4>}}
Let $M$ be a Noetherian $A$-module and let $\mathfrak{a}$ be the annihilator of $M$ in $A$. Prove that $A/\mathfrak{a}$ is a Noetherian ring.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Noetherian module must be finitely generated, so assume $M$ is generated by $x_1,\ldots,x_n$; let $N_i=\operatorname{Ann}(x_i)$, then $A/N_i\simeq Ax_i$ is a submodule of $M$ hence Noether. Repeatedly using Exercise 3 we know $A/\mathfrak{a}=A/\bigcap N_i$ is a Noether $A$-module, hence also as a module on itself, i.e. a Noetherian ring.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
If we replace "Noetherian" by "Artinian" in this result, is it still true?
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
No. The difference is that Artinian modules are not necessarily finitely generated; for example consider $\mathbf{Z}$-module $\mathbf{Z}_p$ (localization at the element $p$). Its submodules are all of the form $\mathbf{Z}[p^{-n}]$, so Artinian; but its annihilator is $0$, and $\mathbf{Z}/0=\mathbf{Z}$ is not Artinian.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 5>}}
A topological space $X$ is said to be *Noetherian* if the open subsets of $X$ satisfy the ascending chain condition (or, equivalently, the maximal condition). Since closed subsets are complements of open subsets, it comes to the same thing to say that the closed subsets of $X$ satisfy the descending chain condition (or, equivalently, the minimal condition). Show that, if $X$ is Noetherian, then every subspace of $X$ is Noetherian, and that $X$ is quasi-compact.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
For subspace $Y$ and chain of open subsets $V_1\subseteq V_2\subseteq\cdots$, take any open sets $U_i$ of $X$ with $U_i\cap Y=V_i$, then the chain \[\bigcup_{i=1}^1U_i\subseteq\bigcup_{i=1}^2U_i\subseteq\cdots\] stabilizes in $X$, hence $V_i$ stabilizes in $Y$.\
For an open cover $\mathcal{O}$ of $X$, consider the set of open subsets \[\mathsf{Fin}_\mathcal{O}=\{\text{finite unions of open subsets in }\mathcal{O}\}\] it must have a maximal element. But $\mathcal{O}$ is a covering, so the maximal element must be $X$, hence $X$ is quasi-compact.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 6>}}
Prove that the following are equivalent:\
i) $X$ is Noetherian.\
ii) Every open subspace of $X$ is quasi-compact.\
iii) Every subspace of $X$ is quasi-compact.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
i) $\Rightarrow$ iii) is Exercise 5. iii) $\Rightarrow$ ii) is trivial.\
ii) $\Rightarrow$ i): for chain of open subsets $U_1\subseteq U_2\subseteq\cdots$, the cover $\{U_i\}$ of the open subset $\bigcup U_i$ has a finite sub-cover, hence the chain stabilizes.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 7>}}
A Noetherian space is a finite union of irreducible closed subspaces. [Consider the set $\Sigma$ of closed subsets of $X$ which are not finite unions of irreducible closed subspaces.]
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
If $\Sigma$ is non-empty, then it has a minimal element $Y$. By definition it cannot be irreducible, so is the union of two proper closed subsets $Y_1$ and $Y_2$, which must be finite unions of irreducible closed subspaces by minimality, hence $Y$ is also in $\Sigma$, contradiction. Hence $\Sigma=\varnothing$; in particular, the total space itself is a finite union of irreducible closed subspaces.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
Hence the set of irreducible components of a Noetherian space is finite.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Express the total space $X$ as a union of irreducible closed subspaces $Y_i(1\le i\le n)$, then each $Y_i$ is contained in some irreducible component $Y_i'$ of $X$. For an irreducible component $Y$, take smallest $I\subseteq\{1,2,\ldots,n\}$ such that $Y\subseteq\bigcup_{i\in I}Y_i$; if $|I|\ge2$, then $Y=\bigcup_{i\in I}(Y\cap Y_i)$ makes $Y$ into union of proper closed subsets contradicting irreducibility, hence $|I|=1$ and $Y$ is one of the $Y_i'$. Thus the set of irreducible components is finite.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 8>}}
If $A$ is a Noetherian ring then $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is a Noetherian topological space. Is the converse true?
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
As $V(\mathfrak{a})\subseteq V(\mathfrak{b})\Leftrightarrow r(\mathfrak{a}\supseteq\mathfrak{b})$, d.c.c. on closed subsets in $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ comes directly from a.c.c. on ideals in $A$.\
The converse is false because there can be a lot of ideals not of the form $r(\cdot)$. For example $A=k[t_1,t_2,\ldots]/(t_1^2,t_2^2,\ldots)$, where $k$ is a field; it has a unique prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}=(t_1,t_2,\ldots)$ which is not finitely generated, hence $A$ is not Noetherian.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 11>}}
Let $f:A\to B$ be a rig homomorphism and suppose that $\operatorname{Spec}(B)$ is a Noetherian space (Exercise 5). Prove that $f^*:\operatorname{Spec}(B)\to\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is a closed mapping if and only if $f$ has the going-up property (Chapter 5, Exercise 10).
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The going-up property only means $f^*(V(\mathfrak{q}))$ is closed for prime ideals $\mathfrak{q}$, while closed mapping requires it to be true for all ideals $\mathfrak{q}$.\
We only need $\Leftarrow$: take any ideal $\mathfrak{b}$, notice that $\operatorname{Spec}(B/\mathfrak{b})$ is a subspace of a Noetherian space hence Noetherian, hence there are only finitely many prime ideals $\mathfrak{q}_i$ minimal in those containing $\mathfrak{b}$. Now $V(\mathfrak{b})=\bigcup V(\mathfrak{q}_i)$, hence its image is a finite union of closed subsets hence closed.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 12>}}
Let $A$ be a ring such that $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is a Noetherian space. Show that the set of prime ideals of $A$ satisfies the ascending chain condition. 
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
For an ascending chain of prime ideals $\mathfrak{p}_1\subseteq\mathfrak{p}_2\subseteq\cdots$, the corresponding closed subsets $V(\mathfrak{p}_1)\supseteq V(\mathfrak{p}_2)\supseteq\cdots$ must stabilize, e.g. $V(\mathfrak{p}_n)=V(\mathfrak{p}_{n+1})=\cdots$, then $\mathfrak{p}_n\in V(\mathfrak{p}_{n+k})\Rightarrow\mathfrak{p}_{n+k}\subseteq\mathfrak{p}_n$ for all $k\ge0$, i.e. the prime ideal chain stabilizes.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
Is the converse true?
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The converse is false. For example consider $A=k^\infty$ the countable infinite product of a field $k$, whose spectrum is a discrete infinite space hence not Noetherian; but every prime ideal is maximal, so a.c.c. on prime ideals holds.
{{</exercise>}}
