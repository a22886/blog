{{<exercise 1>}}
Let $\mathfrak{q}_1\cap\cdots\cap\mathfrak{q}_n=0$ be a minimal primary decomposition of the zero ideal in a Noetherian ring, and let $\mathfrak{q}_i$ be $\mathfrak{p}_i$-primary. Let $\mathfrak{p}_i^{(r)}$ be the $r$th *symbolic power* of $\mathfrak{p}_i$ (Chapter 4, Exercise 13). Show that for each $i=1,\ldots,n$ there exists an integer $r_i$ such that $\mathfrak{p}_i^{(r_i)}\subseteq\mathfrak{q}_i$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
By Proposition 7.14 there is some $r_i$ such that $\mathfrak{p}_i^{r_i}\subseteq\mathfrak{q}_i$, hence $\mathfrak{p}_i^{(r_i)}=S_{\mathfrak{p}_i}(\mathfrak{p}_i^{r_i})\subseteq S_{\mathfrak{p}_i}(\mathfrak{q}_i)=\mathfrak{q}_i$.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
Suppose $\mathfrak{q}_i$ is an isolated primary component. Thn $A_{\mathfrak{p}_i}$ is an Artin local ring, hence if $\mathfrak{m}_i$ is its maximal ideal we have $\mathfrak{m}_i^r=0$ for all sufficiently large $r$, hence $\mathfrak{q}_i=\mathfrak{p}_i^{(r)}$ for all large $r$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
As $\mathfrak{p}_i$ is a minimal prime ideal {{<explain>}}Proposition 4.6{{</explain>}}, there is only one prime ideal in $A_{\mathfrak{p}_i}$. Theorem 8.5 says that it is an Artin local ring.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
If $\mathfrak{q}_i$ is an embedded primary component, then $A_{\mathfrak{p}_i}$ is *not* Artinian, hence the powers $\mathfrak{m}_i^r$ are all distinct, and so the $\mathfrak{p}_i{(r)}$ are all distinct. Hence in the given primary decomposition we can replace $\mathfrak{q}_i$ by any of the infinite set of $\mathfrak{p}_i$-primary ideals $\mathfrak{p}_i^{(r)}$ where $r\ge r_i$, and so there are infinitely many minimal primary decompositions of 0 which differ only in the $\mathfrak{p}_i$-component.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The localization is not Artin because $\dim A_{\mathfrak{p}_i}\neq0$.
{{</exercise>}}

Let $A$ be a Noetherian ring. Prove that the following are equivalent:\
i) $A$ is Artinian;\
ii) $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is discrete and finite;\
iii) $\operatorname{Spec}(A)$ is discrete.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Carefully observing Theorem 8.5 we see i) $\Rightarrow$ ii) $\Rightarrow$ iii) $\Rightarrow$ i).

{{<exercise 4>}}
Let $f:A\to B$ be a ring homomorphism of finite type. Consider the following statements:\
i) $f$ is finite;\
ii) the fibres of $f^*$ are discrete subspaces of $\operatorname{Spec}(B)$;\
iii) for each prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ of $A$, the ring $B\otimes_A k(\mathfrak{p})$ is a finite $k(\mathfrak{p})$-algebra ($k(\mathfrak{p})$ is the residue field of $A_\mathfrak{p}$);\
iv) the fibres of $f^*$ are finite.\
Prove that i) $\Rightarrow$ ii) $\Leftrightarrow$ iii) $\Rightarrow$ iv). [Use Exercises 2 and 3.]
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The fibres of $f^*$ are of the form $\operatorname{Spec}(B\otimes_A k(\mathfrak{p}))$, and for finite $f$ we have $B\otimes_A k(\mathfrak{p})$ is a finitely generated $k(\mathfrak{p})$-algebra, hence Noether. Together with Exercises 2 and 3 we have the desired implications.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
If $f$ is integral and the fibres of $f^*$ are finite, is $f$ necessarily finite?
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
No, for example the embedding of $\mathbf{Q}$ into its algebraic closure.
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 5>}}
In Chapter 5, Exercise 16, show that $X$ is a finite covering of $L$ (i.e., the number of points of $X$ lying over a given point of $L$ is finite and bounded).
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Let $A$ and $A'$ be the coordinate rings of $X$ and $k^r$, and we can see $A'$ as a subring of $A$; identifying them as the maximum spectrum of the coordinate rings we only need to show that there is some $n$ such that for every $\mathfrak{m}\in\operatorname{Max}(A')$ there are no more than $n$ maximal ideals of $A$ lying over $\mathfrak{m}$.\
Recall that $A'\to A$ is of finite type and integral, hence finite; let $A$ be generated by $n$ elements on $A'$, so that $\dim_{k(\mathfrak{p})}A\otimes_{A'}k(\mathfrak{p})\le n$ for prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ of $A'$. Denote $B=A\otimes_{A'}k(\mathfrak{p})$, it suffices to prove that $\operatorname{Spec}(B)$ has at most $n$ points.\
Now $B$ is a finite algebra over a field hence Artin, having all prime ideals maximal and coprime. There results in a surjection $B\to\prod\limits_{\mathfrak{q}\in\operatorname{Spec}(B)}B/\mathfrak{q}$, hence \[n\ge\dim_{k(\mathfrak{p})}B\ge\dim_{k(\mathfrak{p})}\prod_{\mathfrak{q}\in\operatorname{Spec}(B)}B/\mathfrak{q}=\sum_{\mathfrak{q}\in\operatorname{Spec}(B)}\dim_{k(\mathfrak{p})}B/\mathfrak{q}\ge|\operatorname{Spec}(B)|\]
{{</exercise>}}

{{<exercise 6>}}
Let $A$ be a Noetherian ring and $\mathfrak{q}$ a $\mathfrak{p}$-primary ideal in $A$. Consider chains of primary ideals from $\mathfrak{q}$ to $\mathfrak{p}$. Show that all such chins are of finite bounded length, and that all maximal chains have the same length.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
By the ideal correspondence we need only consider the local ring $B=(A/\mathfrak{q})_\mathfrak{p}$ and its $\mathfrak{m}=(\mathfrak{p}/\mathfrak{q})_\mathfrak{p}$-primary ideals. By Proposition 7.14 there is some $n$ such that $\mathfrak{p}^n\subseteq\mathfrak{q}$, i.e. $\mathfrak{m}^n=0$, hence $B$ is Artin, and every ideal is $\mathfrak{m}$-primary {{<explain>}}Corollary 7.16{{</explain>}}. The claim reduces to the chains of ideals in Artin rings: lengths are obviously bounded, and maximal chains are composition chains hence of the same length by Proposition 6.7.
{{</exercise>}}
