{{<exercise 1>}}
Let $A$ be a non-Noetherian ring and let $\Sigma$ be th set of ideals in $A$ which are not finitely generated. Show that $\Sigma$ has maximal elements and that the maximal elements of $\Sigma$ are prime ideals.\
[Let $\mathfrak{a}$ be a maximal element of $\Sigma$, and suppose that there exist $x,y\in A$ such that $x\not\in\mathfrak{a}$ and $y\not\in\mathfrak{a}$ and $xy\in\mathfrak{a}$. Show that there exists a finitely generated ideal $\mathfrak{a}_0\subseteq\mathfrak{a}$ such that $\mathfrak{a}_0+(x)=\mathfrak{a}+(x)$, and that $\mathfrak{a}=\mathfrak{a}_0+x\cdot(\mathfrak{a}:x)$. Since $(\mathfrak{a}:x)$ strictly contains $\mathfrak{a}$, it is finitely generated and therefore so is $\mathfrak{a}$.]
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The hint is basically the full proof. The existence of maximal elements is a direct consequence of Zorn's lemma. Following the hint, For maximal element $\mathfrak{a}$ and $x,y\in A\backslash\mathfrak{a}$, $xy\in\mathfrak{a}$, by maximality $\mathfrak{a}+(x)$ is finitely generated; let its generators be $a_i+b_ix$ where $a_i\in\mathfrak{a}$ and $b_i\in A$. Therefore for $\mathfrak{a}_0=(a_1,\ldots,a_n)$ we have $\mathfrak{a}=\mathfrak{a}_0+x\cdot(\mathfrak{a}:x)$. The hint finishes the proof.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
Hence a ring in which every prime ideal is finitely generated is Noetherian (I. S. Cohen).

{{<exercise 3>}}
Let $\mathfrak{a}$ be an irreducible ideal in a ring $A$. Thn the following are equivalent:\
i) $\mathfrak{a}$ is primary;\
ii) for every multiplicatively closed subset $S$ of $A$ we have $(S^{-1}\mathfrak{a})^c=(\mathfrak{a}:x)$ for some $x\in S$;\
iii) the sequence $(\mathfrak{a}:x^n)$ is stationary, for every $x\in A$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Proposition 4.8 says that the contraction of the extension of a primary ideal in a localization ring is either itself or $(1)$, hence i) $\Rightarrow$ ii). Take $S=\{1,x,x^2,\ldots\}$ we see ii) $\Rightarrow$ iii).\
For iii) $\Rightarrow$ i), let $xy\in\mathfrak{a}$ and $y\not\in\mathfrak{a}$. Then there exists an $n$ such that $tx^{n+1}\in\mathfrak{a}\Rightarrow tx^n\in\mathfrak{a}$. For any $a\in (x^n)\cap (y)$, let $a=by=cx^n$, then $cx^{n+1}=bxy\in\mathfrak{a}\Rightarrow a=cx^n\in\mathfrak{a}$, hence it is clear $\mathfrak{a}=(x^n)\cap (y)+\mathfrak{a}=((x^n)+\mathfrak{a})\cap((y)+\mathfrak{a})$. By irreducibility we must have $x^n\in\mathfrak{a}$.

{{<exercise 4>}}
Which of the following rings are Noetherian?
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
i) The ring of rational functions of $z$ having no pole on the circle $|z|=1$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Let $S=\mathbf{C}[z]\backslash\limits_{|w|=1}(z-w)$, then the ring in question is $S^{-1}\mathbf{C}[z]$ which is Noetherian {{<explain>}}Proposition 7.3, Theorem 7.5{{</explain>}}.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
ii) The ring of power series in $z$ with a positive radius of convergence.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The units are exactly those with nonzero constant terms, hence each $(f)$ is some $(z^m)$, from which a.c.c. is obviously satisfied.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iii) The ring of power series in $z$ with an infinite radius of convergence.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
It is not Noetherian; the difference with ii) is that some $(f)=(g)$ may be true there but not here; e.g. take \[f_n(z)=\prod_{j=n}^{\infty}(1-\frac z{j!})\] then $(f_n)$ is a non-stationary ascending chain.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iv) The ring of polynomials in $z$ whose first $k$ derivatives vanish at the origin ($k$ being a fixed integer).
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The ring in question is $\mathbf{C}+(z^{n+1})$, which is a $\mathbf{C}[z^{n+1}]\simeq\mathbf{C}[z]$-module generated by $\{1,z^{n+2},\ldots,z^{2n+1}\}$, hence Noether by Proposition 7.2.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
v) The ring of polynomials in $z$, $w$ ll of whose partial derivatives with respect to $w$ vanish for $z\neq0$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The ring in question is $\mathbf{C}[z]+(z)\mathbf{C}[w]$. The ascending chain $(zw)\subsetneq(zw,zw^2)\subsetneq(zw,zw^2,zw^3)\subsetneq\cdots$ does not stabilize.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
In all cases the coefficients are complex numbers.

### *Grothendieck groups*

{{<exercise 26>}}
Let $A$ be a Noetherian ring and let $F(A)$ denote the set of all isomorphism classes of finitely generated $A$-modules. Let $C$ be the free abelian group generated by $F(A)$. With each short exact sequence $0\to M'\to M\to M''\to0$ of finitely generated $A$-modules we associate the element $(M')-(M)+(M'')$ of $C$, where $(M)$ is the isomorphism class of $M$, etc. Let $D$ be the subgroup of $C$ generated by these elements, for all short exact sequences. The quotient group $C/D$ is called the *Grothendieck group* of $A$, and is denoted by $K(A)$. If $M$ is a finitely generated $A$-module, let $\gamma(M)$, or $\gamma_A(M)$, denote the image of $(M)$ in $K(A)$.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
i) Show that $K(A)$ has the following universal property: for each additive function $\lambda$ on the class of finitely generated $A$-modules, with values in an abelian group $G$, there exists a unique homomorphism $\lambda_0:K(A)\to G$ such that $\lambda(M)=\lambda_0(\gamma(M))$ for all $M$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Obvious.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
ii) Show that $K(A)$ is generated by the elements $\gamma(A/\mathfrak{p})$, where $\mathfrak{p}$ is a prime ideal of $A$. [Use Exercise 18.]
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
Obvious by Exercise 18.
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
iii) If $A$ is a field, or more generally if $A$ is a principal ideal domain, then $K(A)\simeq \mathbf{Z}$.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
For non-zero prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}=(p)$ there is an exact sequence $0\to A\xrightarrow{p} A\to A/\mathfrak{p}\to0$, hence $\gamma(A/\mathfrak{p})=0$. By ii) $K(A)$ is generated by $\gamma(A)$. It suffices to prove that $K(A)$ has no torsion. The finitely generated modules $M$ over $A$ are all of the form $A^{\operatorname{rank}(M)}\oplus T(M)$ where $T(M)$ is the torsion submodule; tensor with $\operatorname{Frac}(A)$ we see $\operatorname{rank}(\cdot)$ is additive hence induces $K(A)\to\mathbf{Z}_+$ which is clearly not the zero map, thus $K(A)\simeq\mathbf{Z}$.

QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
The main reasons are that finitely generated $B$-modules are also finitely generated as $A$-modules, and exact sequences are still exact.

iv) Let $f:A\to B$ be a ring homomorphism, $B$ being Noetherian. Show that extension of scalars gives rise to a ring homomorphism $f^!:K_1(A)\to K_1(B)$ such tht $f^!(\gamma_1(M))=\gamma_1(B\otimes_A M)$. [If $M$ is flat and finitely generated over $A$, then $B\otimes_A M$ is flat and finitely generated over $B$.] If $g:B\to C$ is another ring homomorphism (with $C$ Noetherian), then $(f\circ g)^!=f^!\circ g^!$.

TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
v) If $f:A\to B$ is a finite ring homomorphism then \[f_!(f^!(x)y)=xf_!(y)\] for $x\in K_1(A)$, $y\in K(B)$. In other words,regarding $K(B)$ as a $K_1(A)$-module by restriction of scalars, the homomorphism $f^!$ is a $K_1(A)$-module homomorphism.
QUESTIONANDANSWERSTRING\
As $K_1(A)$ and $K(B)$ are generated by the $\gamma_{1,A}(M)$ and $\gamma_B(N)$, it suffices to prove the claim for them: we have \[\begin{align*}f_!(f^!(\gamma_{1,A}(M))\gamma_B(N))&=f_!(\gamma_{1,B}(B\otimes_A M)\gamma_B(N))\\&=f_!(\gamma_B(N\otimes_B B\otimes_A M))=\gamma_A(M\otimes_A N)\end{align*}\] where on the right $M\otimes_A N$ is seen as an $A$-module, which is equivalent to seeing $N$ as an $A$-module and then tensor with $M$, hence \[\gamma_A(M\otimes_A N)=\gamma_{1,A}(M)\gamma_A(N)=\gamma_{1,A}(M)f_!(\gamma_B(N))\]
TOMANDJERRYSTRINGLITERAL\
*Remark*. Since $F_1(A)$ is a subset of $F(A)$ we have a group homomorphism $\epsilon:K_1(A)\to K(A)$, given by $\epsilon(\gamma_1(M))=\gamma(M)$. If the ring $A$ is finite-dimensional  and *regular*, i.e., if all its local rings $A_\mathfrak{p}$ are regular (Chapter 11) it can be shown that $\epsilon$ is an isomorphism.
